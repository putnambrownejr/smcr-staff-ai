## Checking In to a New Unit

Walk Marines through every step of reporting to a new SMCR unit:

### Phase 1 — Before First Drill (T-30 to T-7)

**Admin preparation:**
- Contact the unit S-1 or I&I admin chief. Get the check-in package.
- Verify orders are correct: unit, billet, reporting date.
- Ensure SRB/OMPF is transferred and current. Missing docs = delayed pay.
- Update SGLI beneficiary (SOES online or form SGLV 8286).
- Update Page 2 (Record of Emergency Data, NAVMC 10922).
- Verify dental readiness — get to Class 1 or 2 before reporting if possible.
- Complete PHA if due within 90 days.
- Ensure DBIDS (base access) is current or arrange a visitor pass.

**System enrollments to verify:**
- MOL (Marine Online) — verify access, correct unit showing
- Drill Manager — new unit must add you for IDT pay
- DTS (Defense Travel System) — new unit routing list and approving official
- MROWS — verify the new unit can place you on orders
- MarineNet — training records should follow you, but verify
- Unit communication channels — email distro, group chat, etc.

**Documents to bring (originals or certified copies):**
- Military ID (CAC)
- Orders to the unit
- Certified copies of recent FitReps or ProfReps
- Training certificates (PME completions, MOS schools, ranges)
- Medical/dental records or proof of current readiness
- SGLI form
- Page 2 (Record of Emergency Data)
- Driver's license (for base access if DBIDS not yet set up)

### Phase 2 — Formal Reporting

**Officer reporting:**
- Formally report to the CO. Service Alphas unless told otherwise.
- Prepare a 60-second intro: name, rank, MOS, where you're from, what you bring.
- You may also report to the XO and your section head/OIC.
- Request your reporting senior's expectations for your first FitRep period.
- Ask: "What does right look like in this billet for the first 90 days?"

**Enlisted reporting:**
- Report to your section SNCO or platoon sergeant first, then the 1stSgt/SgtMaj.
- The 1stSgt may bring you before the CO, or the CO may see new joins at formation.
- Know your MOS, last duty station; be ready for questions about training and quals.
- Don't oversell — be honest about what you know and don't know.

**Lateral move / transfer notes:**
- Ensure your losing unit completed checkout and service record transfer.
- OMPF and SRB should be current — missing docs from the old unit will persist.
- If transferring between MSCs (4th MarDiv → 4th MLG, etc.), expect extra admin lag.
- Drill points and retirement year may need manual verification after transfer.

### Phase 3 — Admin & Orientation (First Drill)

- Complete all check-in paperwork with S-1.
- Verify Drill Manager shows you for IDT pay — no entry, no paycheck.
- Get unit recall roster info and emergency contact procedures.
- Identify your chain of command, duty section, and key points of contact.
- Tour the drill site: armory, classrooms, parking, medical, chow.
- Ask about unit SOPs: liberty policy, uniform of the day, comms plan, PT standards.
- Find out what the unit uses for between-drill communication (email, group chat, etc.).

### Phase 4 — First 90 Days

- Meet your Marines (if in a leadership billet) or your immediate chain.
- Get a battle rhythm: drill dates, AT dates, key training events for the year.
- Establish your DTS profile and submit any outstanding travel claims.
- Start working toward your billet requirements (MOS quals, PME, ranges).
- If officer: get your first counseling/expectations session from your reporting senior.
- If SNCO: meet with the 1stSgt/SgtMaj to understand unit priorities.
- Complete any overdue annual training (Cyber Awareness, TARP, SAPR, etc.).
- Verify your PME status — are you eligible or overdue for the next level?

## 5-Paragraph Order Coaching

Even if you never write an OPORD from scratch, understanding the 5-paragraph order
format helps you read orders, write FRAGOs, brief clearly, and think about problems
the way the Marine Corps expects. This section teaches the format and the thinking
behind it.

### The Format (MCWP 5-10)

**1. SITUATION** — What's going on?
- **Enemy forces:** What the threat is doing, what they're capable of, and what they
  probably want. In a training context, describe the scenario threat.
- **Friendly forces:** What higher is doing, what units are to your left and right,
  what support you have.
- **Attachments and detachments:** What's been added to or taken from your unit.

*The point:* Everyone reading this paragraph should understand the world they're
operating in. If someone reads only this paragraph, they should know whether this
is a big deal or routine.

**2. MISSION** — One sentence. Who, what, when, where, and why.

Example:
> 2d Platoon, Bravo Company attacks 261400ZJUN26 to seize OBJ HAWK (grid 12345678)
> in order to deny the enemy observation of MSR TAMPA.

*The point:* This is the sentence the entire order exists to support. If you can't
write it in one sentence, you don't understand your own mission yet. The "in order to"
clause (the purpose) is the most important part — it tells subordinates what matters
if the plan falls apart.

**3. EXECUTION** — How we're going to do it.
- **Commander's intent:** End state, purpose, key tasks. What must happen even if
  nothing else goes according to plan.
- **Concept of operations:** The scheme of maneuver — who does what in what order.
  This is the "movie" of the operation.
- **Tasks to subordinate units:** Specific tasks assigned to each element.
  "1st Squad: establish support-by-fire position NLT 261345ZJUN26."
- **Coordinating instructions:** Timeline, control measures, ROE, PIR, priorities.
  Everything that keeps elements from running into each other.

*The point:* This is where most of the thinking lives. A good paragraph 3 gives
subordinates enough direction to act, enough flexibility to adapt, and enough
coordination to not shoot each other.

**4. ADMIN & LOGISTICS** — Beans, bullets, and bandaids.
- Supply: ammo, water, chow, fuel — what you have and how to get more
- Transportation: how things move
- Services: maintenance, mortuary affairs, EPW handling
- Personnel: replacements, casualty reporting
- CASEVAC/MEDEVAC: collection points, routes, priorities

*The point:* Nobody wants to read this paragraph, but operations that fail usually
fail here. If the logistics plan doesn't support the scheme of maneuver, the scheme
of maneuver is fiction.

**5. COMMAND & SIGNAL** — Who's in charge and how we talk.
- Command: where the commander will be, succession of command
- Signal: frequencies, call signs, challenge/password, pyrotechnics, signals
  (Note: in an UNCLASSIFIED environment, use training-only comms data — never
  real frequencies or COMSEC)

*The point:* When things go wrong, people need to know who to call and how to
reach them. This paragraph answers both.

### Common OPORD Mistakes

- **Mission statement doesn't have a purpose.** "Attack to seize OBJ HAWK" is
  incomplete. "... in order to deny enemy observation of MSR TAMPA" tells
  subordinates why it matters.
- **Concept of operations is a task list, not a concept.** The concept should
  paint a picture of how the fight unfolds. "1st Squad does X, then 2d Squad
  does Y" is a task list. "We fix the enemy with fires, then envelop from the
  south" is a concept.
- **Coordinating instructions are missing.** Boundaries, phase lines, fire
  coordination measures, time hacks — if these aren't in the order, elements
  will make them up on the fly.
- **Admin & Logistics is an afterthought.** If you can't explain how ammo gets
  forward and casualties get rearward, the plan isn't finished.
- **The order is too long.** A company OPORD should be 2-4 pages. If yours is
  10 pages, you're writing doctrine, not an order. Keep it to what subordinates
  need to execute.

### WARNO vs OPORD vs FRAGO

- **WARNO (Warning Order):** "Heads up, we're going to do something. Start
  preparing." Uses the same 5-paragraph format but abbreviated — you don't
  have all the information yet, and that's fine. The point is to give
  subordinates time to prepare.
- **OPORD (Operation Order):** The full order. Everything needed to execute.
- **FRAGO (Fragmentary Order):** "Change to the existing order." Only state
  what's changing — don't rewrite the whole order. If the timeline shifts
  but the concept is the same, the FRAGO only addresses the timeline.

### Worked Example — Paragraph 2 (Mission)

**Bad:**
> "The platoon will conduct operations in the training area."

Why it's bad: No specificity, no purpose, no time, no endstate.

**Better:**
> "2d Platoon conducts a zone reconnaissance 271600ZJUN26 to 280400ZJUN26
> along Axis BLUE in order to locate enemy defensive positions and report
> findings to Bravo Company."

Why it's better: Who (2d Plt), what (zone recon), when (time window), where
(Axis BLUE), why (locate enemy, report to higher).

### How to Coach Someone Writing Their First Order

1. Start with paragraph 2 (Mission). If they can't write the mission in one
   sentence, they're not ready to write the rest.
2. Then paragraph 3 (Execution) — make them explain the concept out loud
   before they write it down. If they can't explain it in 30 seconds, it's
   too complicated.
3. Then paragraph 1 (Situation) — this grounds the order in reality.
4. Then paragraph 4 (Admin & Logistics) — force them to answer: "How does
   ammo get forward? How do casualties get out?"
5. Last, paragraph 5 (Command & Signal) — the simplest to write if the
   rest is solid.

## Uniform Standards

Guidance grounded in **MCO 1020.34H** (Marine Corps Uniform Regulations).

**Service uniforms:**
- Service A: coat + ribbons (formal occasions)
- Service B: long-sleeve shirt + tie
- Service C: short-sleeve shirt, no tie (daily garrison)

**Dress uniforms:**
- Blue Dress A: medals
- Blue Dress B: ribbons
- Blue Dress C: shirt + ribbons
- Blue Dress D: shirt, no ribbons
- Evening Dress: formal events

**Seasonal changeover:**
- Summer whites/short-sleeve: 1 May – 30 Sep
- Winter greens/long-sleeve: 1 Oct – 30 Apr
- Local CG may adjust +/- 30 days — confirm before drill

**Common uniform occasions:**

| Occasion | Typical Uniform | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Formation / muster | Service C or MCCUU | Per unit SOP |
| Promotion ceremony | Service A or Blue Dress B | CO discretion |
| Birthday Ball | Blue Dress A or Evening Dress | MARADMIN specifies |
| AT field ops | MCCUU (woodland/desert per CG) | Full combat load |
| PFT/CFT | Green-on-green PT gear | Seasonal cold-wx allowed |
| Travel to/from drill | Civilian attire (unless directed) | Reserve SOP |

**MCCUU/MARPAT rules:**
- Woodland vs desert based on CG guidance
- No starching. Sleeves down (never cuffed). Boot bands required.
- Name tapes, rank, EGA placement per MCO 1020.34H Chapter 5

**Grooming standards:**
- Hair above collar (male), secured above collar (female)
- Mustache within corners of mouth, no other facial hair
- Bulk standards per MCO 1020.34H

**Ribbon precedence order:**
- Personal decorations (senior to junior): Navy Cross, Silver Star, Bronze Star, etc.
- Unit awards: PUC, MUC, etc.
- Campaign and service awards
- Foreign awards (last)
- Verify current MARADMIN for any precedence changes

**Pre-drill uniform checklist:**
- Confirm uniform of the day (check drill notice / I&I guidance)
- Inspect serviceability: loose threads, stains, missing buttons, tarnished brass
- Verify rank, ribbon, and insignia placement
- Check grooming standard compliance
- Ensure all items fit properly — nothing says "I only wear this once a month" like
  a uniform that doesn't fit

**Reserve-specific uniform notes:**
- Uniform allowance timing varies — verify when yours hits
- Sea bag inspections often happen during AT
- Civilian attire is generally authorized for travel to/from drill unless directed otherwise
- Local I&I may publish a uniform of the day that differs from the parent command

## Drill Prep

**Key policy baselines:**
- 48 IDT + 14 days AT per year (MCO 1001R.1L w/CH-2, 7 Mar 2025)
- MARADMIN 157/25: $750 IDT travel reimbursement cap
- AT planning triggers: T-45 (MROWS submission), T-30 (DTS authorization), T-15 (final coordination)

**Admin systems Marines need to know:**
- **Drill Manager** — captures IDT attendance → drives pay. Errors delay the entire pay cycle.
- **MROWS** — generates ADT/AT orders. Must be submitted with enough lead time for approval.
- **DTS** — travel vouchers. Post-drill voucher completion is the most common drop.
- **MOL** — self-service for LES, OMPF, training records. Verify before drill.
- **MCTFS/Unit Diary** — authoritative system of record for all status changes.
- **MRRS/RHRP/PHA** — medical readiness tracking (dental, PHA, IMR must be current).

**Pre-drill (T-14 to T-1):**
- Confirm drill date, travel requirements, must-hit suspense items
- Verify MOL access, LES accuracy, medical readiness
- Check DTS: prior voucher settled? New authorization needed?
- Uniform, gear, required annual training (MarineNet) complete
- MROWS: any ADT/AT orders pending approval?

**During drill:**
- Drill Manager attendance captured accurately (drives pay)
- Capture action items while the right people are present
- FitRep counseling, awards routing, admin corrections — do it now or wait 28 days

**Post-drill (release + 5 days):**
- DTS voucher completion (most common drop)
- Handoff notes updated for continuity between drills
- Follow up on any admin actions that require between-drill work

**Reserve friction points to front-load:**
- Asynchronous admin between drills — things break during the 28-day gap
- Dual-status civilians with employer notification requirements (USERRA)
- Geographic dispersion — Marines traveling from multiple states
- System fragmentation across 6+ platforms with different access requirements
- Compressed training year — 48 IDT periods to accomplish a full training plan

**Navy personnel attached to Marine units (corpsmen, chaplains, RPs):**
- Navy reservists' orders go through their NOSC via NROWS, NOT MROWS
- The Marine unit is the gaining command — you request, the NOSC executes
- Start NROWS requests at T-60 minimum (vs T-45 for Marine MROWS)
- Pay issues for Navy personnel route through the NOSC, not the Marine S-1

## CAC / PKI Troubleshooting

### Step 1 — Identify the Problem Type

Before diving into fixes, figure out which layer is failing:

| Symptom | Likely Layer | Start Here |
|---|---|---|
| Computer doesn't see the card at all | Hardware / Reader | Reader & hardware checks |
| Card detected but no certificate prompt | Middleware | Middleware checks |
| Certificate prompt appears but wrong cert or login fails | Certificate / Browser | Certificate checks |
| One portal fails, others work | Portal-specific | Portal checks |
| Signing/encrypting email fails | Signing / Encryption | Email client checks |

### Reader & Hardware Issues

**Likely causes:** Reader or cable seating issue, reader driver not loading, CAC
not detected consistently by the host system.

**Immediate checks:**
- Reseat the CAC and reader, then test whether the OS detects the card before
  opening any portal
- Try a different USB port or approved reader if available
- Check whether another smart-card-aware application sees the CAC (e.g., does
  the lock screen offer smart card login?)

**Deeper checks:**
- Validate middleware installation and recent updates
- Confirm the issue persists after a clean reboot
- Compare behavior on a second approved device to separate card issues from
  host issues
- Check reader model and driver compatibility with your OS version

### Middleware Issues

**Likely causes:** Middleware missing, disabled, or stale. Recent OS updates changed
certificate access behavior. Multiple middleware tools competing.

**Approved middleware by platform:**
- Windows (GFE): Usually ActivClient — verify version with your S-6
- Windows (personal): ActivClient or OpenSC
- Mac: CACKey, smart card pairing in Keychain Access
- Linux: OpenSC + PCSC-Lite

**Immediate checks:**
- Confirm the approved middleware is installed and running
- Restart the host and retest after confirming the middleware service loads
- Check whether multiple middleware tools are competing on the same machine
  (this is a common cause of intermittent failures)

**Deeper checks:**
- Validate version against current enterprise guidance (your S-6 should know)
- Remove assumptions about browser problems until middleware behavior is
  confirmed independently

### Certificate Issues

**Likely causes:** Wrong certificate selected for the task. Expired or unavailable
certificate chain. Portal rejecting the certificate.

**Understanding your certificates:**
- Your CAC has multiple certificates on it — typically:
  - **DOD ID** — for authenticating to websites (this is the one you usually want)
  - **DOD EMAIL** — for signing/encrypting email
  - **DOD SW CA** — for software signing (rarely used directly)
- When a portal prompts you to select a certificate, pick the right one for the task
- If no prompt appears at all, the issue is likely middleware or browser, not certificate

**Immediate checks:**
- Confirm whether the task needs authentication (DOD ID), signing (DOD EMAIL),
  or encryption — and choose the matching certificate
- Check the displayed certificate dates — are they expired?
- Test another official CAC-enabled site to see if the issue is portal-specific

**Deeper checks:**
- Validate certificate trust chain in your browser's certificate manager
- Check whether the portal recently changed supported browser or certificate behavior
- Clear SSL state: Windows → Internet Options → Content → Clear SSL State

### Portal-Specific Issues (MOL, myPay, MarineNet, etc.)

**Likely causes:** Portal-specific outage or browser compatibility issue. Certificate
flow works generally but the target site is failing. Network or VPN issue.

**Immediate checks:**
- Confirm whether other CAC-enabled portals work (try 2-3 different ones)
- Check whether the issue is limited to one URL or one workflow on that site
- Try the portal's supported browser (Edge and Chrome work best for most DoD sites)

**Deeper checks:**
- Look for portal maintenance notices or enterprise outages
- Try a private/incognito browser window to rule out cached state
- Compare behavior on different network paths if possible

### Email Signing/Encryption Issues

**Likely causes:** Signing/encryption certificate mismatch. Outlook configuration
drift. Recipient trust or certificate availability issue.

**Immediate checks:**
- Confirm whether the failure is in signing, encrypting, or both
- Test a simple sign-only workflow before troubleshooting encryption
- Verify Outlook can still see the expected signing certificates

**Deeper checks:**
- Separate sender configuration issues from recipient trust issues
- Check whether the problem started after updates to Outlook, browser, or middleware

### Escalation Path

1. Try the self-service steps above
2. Document: what system failed, what browser, which certificate behavior was observed
3. Contact your unit's S-6 or Comm officer
4. Contact your I&I IT support
5. Contact the RAPIDS/DEERS office (for CAC card issues)
6. Contact the Enterprise Service Desk (ESD) for network/portal issues

**When escalating, bring:**
- Device type (GFE vs personal, OS version)
- Browser name and version
- Exact error message or behavior observed
- Whether the issue affects one portal or multiple
- Whether a second device/reader produces the same behavior

**Important:** Never share your PIN, private keys, or screenshots showing certificate
serial numbers when troubleshooting.

## Career Tracking

### PME (Professional Military Education) Progression

**Officer PME track:**
- TBS → complete before assignment to a unit billet
- EWS (Expeditionary Warfare School) — Captain level, now mostly online/blended
- CSC/CSOS (Command and Staff College / College of Distance Education) — Major level
- Top Level School — Colonel level (competitive selection)

**Enlisted PME track:**
- Corporals Course
- Sergeants Course
- Career Course (Staff Sergeant)
- Advanced Course (Gunnery Sergeant and above)

**Reserve-specific PME notes:**
- Many PME courses are available via MarineNet or blended delivery
- Some require resident attendance — plan around drill schedule and AT
- PME completion directly affects promotion eligibility
- Check your PME status on MOL and verify completion dates are recorded correctly

### FitRep / Evaluation Awareness

Even if you're not writing FitReps yet, understanding the system helps you:
- FitReps are occasion-driven (transfer, annual, change of RS), not calendar-driven
- Your reporting senior's profile matters — a 4.4 average from a tight grader is
  better than a 4.8 from someone who marks everyone high
- The RS section (Section I) is what the board reads most carefully
- Reserve FitReps cover longer periods with less observation time — make the most
  of drill weekends and AT
- First counseling should happen within 30 days. If it doesn't, ask for it.

### Billet & Career Discovery

- Check MOL for available billets in your MOS and grade
- Talk to your monitor — they can tell you what billets are open and where
- Consider: geographic preference, command potential billets, joint billets,
  IMA (Individual Mobilization Augmentee) opportunities
- For officers: your OMPF, FitReps, PME, and key billet assignments form
  the record the promotion board sees. Be intentional about what you do next.

## Leadership Framing

For PME discussions, leader development, command climate conversations, and
professional growth.

### How to Think About Leadership Problems

Use these lenses to frame problems differently — each one surfaces something
the others miss:

- **Lejeune lens:** What does this do to institutional trust, stewardship, and
  the long arc of the Corps? What would a leader who's thinking about the next
  generation do here?
- **Krulak lens:** What are you doing to sustain transformation instead of merely
  demanding compliance? How does this make Marines better, not just more obedient?
- **Mattis lens:** What standard are you training to, what assumption is weak, and
  what deserves to be cut? Where is the gap between what we say and what we do?
- **Butler lens:** What habit, vanity, or bureaucracy is being tolerated because
  nobody wants the argument? What are we doing because we've always done it?

### Practical Leadership Application

- Name the decision or leadership problem plainly — no euphemisms
- Identify which Marines are most affected and how
- Pull one doctrinal principle and one historical lesson — not five
- Translate both into one action, one standard, and one follow-up check
- If the problem is really legal, medical, behavioral-health, or disciplinary,
  route it to the right authority early instead of dressing it up as philosophy

### Common Leadership Traps in the Reserve

- **Standards are episodic:** If you only enforce standards at drill, correction
  feels like mood instead of leadership. Build it into the battle rhythm.
- **Values talk only happens at ceremonies:** If leaders only discuss values during
  formal events, the command climate is probably shallower than it sounds.
- **The "one weekend a month" excuse:** Using limited time as a reason not to develop
  Marines. You have 48 IDT periods — that's enough to run a genuine leader
  development program if you prioritize it.
- **Confusing activity with development:** Filling the training schedule with events
  is not the same as developing Marines. Ask: "What is this drill weekend making
  our Marines better at?"

### PME Discussion Design

When planning a PME session:
1. Pick one problem, case study, or doctrinal concept — not three
2. Frame it as a question, not a lecture topic: "When is disobedience justified?"
   beats "Leadership and the chain of command"
3. Provide a short reading (1-3 pages max) ahead of time
4. Plan the discussion around 2-3 questions that force choice and judgment
5. Let Marines argue — the learning is in the disagreement, not the slides

### Recommended Reading (Commandant's Professional Reading List)

- *Warfighting* (MCDP 1) — The foundational text. Read it every year.
- *Leading Marines* (MCRP 1-10.2) — Principles of leadership with case studies
- *With the Old Breed* by E.B. Sledge — Enlisted perspective from the Pacific
- *The Defence of Duffer's Drift* by Swinton — Tactical decision-making through
  repeated failure (short, accessible, perfect for PME discussion)
- *First to Fight* by Victor Krulak — Why the Marine Corps exists and how it
  fights to stay relevant
