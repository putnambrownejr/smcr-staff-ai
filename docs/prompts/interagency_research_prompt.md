# Deep Research Prompt — Interagency & Joint Operations Reference

Paste this into ChatGPT (with deep research / browsing enabled) or any AI
research tool. The output goes into `docs/interagency_reference.md`.

---

Build a comprehensive, UNCLASSIFIED reference document covering how a USMC
reserve unit coordinates with external organizations across three domains:
**joint (other US military branches)**, **interagency (other US government
agencies)**, and **multinational/coalition (allied nations, NGOs, international
organizations)**. This is a practical coordination reference — not doctrine
theory, but "who do I contact, what structure exists, what agreements govern
this, and what do I need to set up."

Structure the document in these sections:

## 1. Joint Operations (Other US Military Branches)

For each branch (Army, Navy/Coast Guard, Air Force/Space Force, National Guard
including Title 10 vs Title 32 distinctions):

- Command relationship options (OPCON, TACON, support) and when each applies
- Liaison officer (LNO) requirements and exchange procedures
- Communications interoperability: common frequencies, PACE plan integration,
  joint comms planning considerations, Link 16 / SADL / other data links
- Logistics: cross-service support agreements (CSSA), who provides what,
  Class I-IX supply chain differences
- Common friction points when Marines work with that branch (different
  terminology, planning process differences, timeline expectations)
- Key joint publications that govern the relationship
- Practical example: a reserve infantry battalion conducting a joint exercise
  with an Army National Guard brigade — what coordination is needed?

## 2. Multinational / Coalition Operations

- STANAG (Standardization Agreement) framework — what it is, key STANAGs a
  Marine unit would encounter
- NATO interoperability basics: staff structure differences (J vs G vs N),
  classification sharing (REL TO, FVEY), comms compatibility
- Coalition communications: HF/VHF interoperability challenges, COMSEC sharing
  limitations, common workarounds
- Status of Forces Agreements (SOFA) — what they cover, who negotiates, what a
  unit-level Marine needs to know
- Technical agreements (TA) and memoranda of understanding (MOU) for exercises
- Bilateral exercise coordination with Canada (CANUS exercises are common for
  reserves — cover Canadian Armed Forces structure, ABCA program, how to
  coordinate a bilateral training event)
- Other common USMC bilateral partners: UK Royal Marines, Australian Defence
  Force, Japanese Self-Defense Forces, Philippine Marines
- Host nation coordination: customs, immigration, base access, local national
  support
- Practical example: a reserve CA team attached to a MEU coordinating with
  Canadian forces and local military in a partner nation — what agreements,
  liaison, and comms setup are needed?

## 3. Interagency (Other US Government Agencies)

- Department of State: embassy structure, Country Team, Defense Attaché Office
  (DAO), Regional Security Officer (RSO), how military units coordinate through
  the embassy
- Current State Dept humanitarian/aid structure (post-2025 USAID dissolution):
  Under Secretary "F", Bureau for Humanitarian Assistance, Bureau of Disaster &
  Humanitarian Response (proposed), PRM, DRL
- Department of Homeland Security / FEMA: domestic disaster response
  coordination (DSCA), dual-status commander concept, Title 10 vs Title 32 for NG
- Intelligence Community: DNI structure, theater intelligence sharing, how
  tactical units request support
- Law enforcement coordination: DEA, FBI, CBP — authorities and limitations for
  military support (counterdrug, counter-trafficking)
- Other agencies Marines might encounter: DOE (nuclear incidents), EPA
  (environmental), HHS (pandemic response), USDA (Food for Peace)
- Practical example: a reserve unit operating in the same AO as Army units in a
  partner nation — how do they coordinate through the embassy, deconflict with
  other agencies, engage local NGOs?

## 4. NGO and International Organization Coordination

- Key international organizations: UN (OCHA, UNHCR, WFP, WHO, UNICEF),
  ICRC/IFRC
- How military units coordinate with humanitarian organizations (civil-military
  coordination centers, CMOC, humanitarian liaison)
- NGO perspectives on military coordination (many NGOs avoid visible military
  association — why, and how to work around it)
- Humanitarian principles (humanity, neutrality, impartiality, independence)
  and why military planners need to understand them
- Common coordination mechanisms: cluster system, humanitarian country team,
  OSOCC
- Practical example: Marines providing logistics support in a disaster zone
  where 15 NGOs are operating — what coordination structures exist?

## 5. Communications & Liaison Quick Reference

- Joint/coalition frequency management and spectrum coordination
- PACE plan template for joint/multinational operations
- Liaison officer checklist: what to bring, what to establish, what to report
- Common call sign and reporting format differences across branches/nations
- Classification and information sharing: FVEY, REL TO, NOFORN practical guide

## Format and Rules

- **UNCLASSIFIED only** — no real frequencies, call signs, COMSEC, or
  classified procedures
- Cite sources by publication number (JP, CJCSI, STANAG, MCO, etc.)
- Focus on practical coordination mechanics, not doctrine theory
- Include realistic examples that a reserve Marine would actually encounter
- When information may have changed, note "confirm current status"
- End each section with a "Key References" list of relevant publications
- Use markdown formatting with headers, tables, and bullet points
- Target length: comprehensive but concise — this is a reference, not a
  textbook. Aim for ~3,000-5,000 words total.
