# Interagency & Joint Operations Reference

**UNCLASSIFIED — Advisory reference only. Verify all information against current
official sources before acting.**

This reference covers practical coordination mechanics for working with external
organizations: other US military branches, allied nations, US government agencies,
and NGOs/international organizations. It answers "who do I contact, what structure
exists, what agreements govern this, and what do I need to set up."

**This is NOT the G-9 / Civil Affairs lane.** G-9 handles the civil estimate,
ASCOPE/PMESII, community engagement, and Annex G. This reference covers the
organizational plumbing — command relationships, liaison, comms interoperability,
agreements, and coordination mechanisms that make joint/interagency/multinational
operations work.

## 1. Joint Operations (Other US Military Branches)

**Army:** The Army is the primary land component; joint operations with a Marine
Reserve unit usually put the Marine unit under *Army OPCON/TACON* or a **support**
relationship. Under Goldwater-Nichols, combatant commanders hold *command authority*
(COCOM) and may delegate operational control (OPCON) or tactical control (TACON) to
subordinates. OPCON allows a commander to organize forces and assign tasks (e.g.
attaching a Marine infantry battalion to an Army brigade), while TACON provides
detailed control of movements (e.g. tasking the Marines' platoons on the
battlefield). Support relationships (general, mutual, direct, close) may also be
assigned. In practice, a Marine reserve battalion attached to an Army brigade keeps
*ADCON* (administrative control) through USMC (for personnel, readiness, training)
while the Army brigade exercises OPCON/TACON for the operation.

- **Liaison (LNO):** Per Army FM 6-0 and JP 3-57, liaison officers are exchanged
  *reciprocally* between higher, supporting, supported, and adjacent organizations.
  The Marine battalion should send an LNO to the Army brigade staff (S-3/S-4) and
  receive an Army LNO at its HQ. The Marine Corps' FLNO/MLNO programs
  institutionalize foreign and Marine liaison exchanges.

- **Communications:** Army and Marine forces both use similar tactical comms (e.g.
  **SINCGARS** VHF-FM radios, **AN/PRC-117G** SATCOM, Link 16 data terminals on
  aviation assets). Planning should integrate these into a joint PACE
  (Primary/Alternate/Contingency/Emergency) comm plan. Data links: Army and Marine
  aviation both use Link 16; Marine F/A-18s and Army helicopters can exchange data
  via **SADL** or J-series networks under JCS direction. Units must coordinate
  through the joint spectrum manager (via J-6 or component) to avoid interference.
  Joint publications (JP 6-0) and CJCSI 6232.01F (Link-16 procedures) govern
  spectrum use.

- **Logistics:** Each service's logistics organization usually supplies its own
  classes of supply, but mutual support is common. Cross-Service Support Agreements
  (CSSAs) or general support arrangements allow one service to provide bulk fuel,
  rations, or transport under predefined terms. Marines often draw food and repair
  parts from DLA and Marine Corps supply stocks; Army uses the Joint Supply System.
  In exercises, a Marine battalion might coordinate with the Army's Theater
  Sustainment Command for ammunition and medical resupply. Acquisition &
  Cross-Servicing Agreements (ACSA) apply internationally; domestically, joint
  logistics coordination follows JP 4-0 and service regulations.

- **Friction points:** Marines and soldiers use different jargon and planning
  processes. Army's MDMP steps vs. Marine Corps Planning Process (MCPP) have
  different timelines and terminology. Cultural differences (Army's emphasis on
  massed fires vs. Marines' amphibious focus) and different publication cycles can
  cause mismatches. Clear liaison and mutual orientation sessions are key.

- **Practical Example:** A reserve infantry battalion joining an Army National Guard
  brigade's exercise would begin by exchanging liaisons (Marine XO or Maj to Army
  G-3, and an Army LNO to the Marine CO). They'd jointly develop a communications
  plan (sharing FM nets and data-link instructions) and a PACE fallback plan.
  Logistics planners would arrange CSSAs: e.g. Army fuels and feeds the Marines
  while Marines share transport assets. The Marines would receive a FRAGORD
  synchronized to the Army OPORD, adapt Marine SOPs to Army formats, and integrate
  into the brigade's battle rhythm.

**Key References:** JP 3-0 (Joint Operations), JP 4-0 (Joint Logistics), JP 6-0
(C4 Systems), JP 3-16 (Multinational Operations), JP 1-02 (DOD Dictionary), FM 6-0
(Army Command & Staff), MCO 5710.7 (Foreign/Marine LNOs).

## 2. Multinational / Coalition Operations

- **STANAG Framework:** NATO uses *Standardization Agreements (STANAGs)* to define
  common interoperability standards. Key STANAGs affecting Marines include military
  symbology (APP-6A/STANAG 2019), map and date formats, ammo calibers,
  communications (e.g. SINCGARS/Narrowband SATCOM), and data links (e.g. MIDS
  Link-16/STANAG 5516, UAV command STANAG 4586). Marines should verify maps,
  markings, and radio waveforms conform to allied STANAGs.

- **NATO Staff & Classification:** Multinational staffs use letter codes: *G-*
  (Army/Marine) or *N-* (Navy) for single-service staffs, *J-* for multi-service
  joint staffs, and *C-* (Combined) for fully multinational staffs (NATO uses
  C1-C6). Allies share information under classification caveats: "REL TO [nations]"
  or "FVEY" (Five Eyes). For example, a US SECRET report releasable to UK, CAN, AUS
  would be marked **SECRET//REL USA UK CAN AUS**. Use REL NATO for NATO-shared,
  NOFORN to restrict.

- **Coalition Communications:** Coalition forces often lack common crypto or radios.
  COMSEC sharing is limited: non-Five Eyes nations typically cannot use US encryption
  devices (e.g. KY-57 VINSON). Workarounds include: planning common unclassified HF
  nets, arranging allied crypto (NATO/Common Crypto Standard), or using wideband
  SATCOM with coalition-approved encryption (e.g. NATO-endorsed TACLANE). Link
  networks like NATO Link-11, Link-16, or civilian satphones may be used. Early
  spectrum coordination through the coalition freq manager or host nation is critical.

- **SOFA (Status of Forces Agreements):** SOFAs define the legal status of US forces
  in a host country (jurisdiction over personnel, customs/tax privileges, etc.).
  These are bilateral or multilateral treaties negotiated by State and DoD, typically
  signed by the US Ambassador and host nation's Minister of Defense. SOFAs cover
  criminal jurisdiction, import/export of military goods, tax exemptions, and base
  rights. Key checks for a Marine unit: entry/exit requirements, ROE regarding local
  law enforcement, and facility rights. For smaller or short-term deployments, a
  Visiting Forces Agreement (VFA) serves similarly.

- **Technical Agreements (TA) & MOUs:** Before a multinational exercise, planners
  sign TAs and MOUs. A TA might specify communications parameters, fuel and
  ammunition exchange terms, or ROE limits. MOUs define training objectives, burden
  sharing, or use of ranges. Templates typically come from higher HQ (e.g. SHAPE or
  service logistics manuals).

- **CANUS (Canada) & ABCA:** The US and Canada regularly train under "CANUS"
  agreements. Canadian Armed Forces structure includes reserve-heavy brigade groups
  that often host joint exercises (e.g. Maple Resolve). Marine planners coordinate
  through US Embassy Ottawa (via the Defense Attache Office) and Canadian unit FOs.
  The ABCA (America, Britain, Canada, Australia) Land Force Standardization Committee
  harmonizes Army/Marine doctrines among those nations. Logistics often flows under a
  Temporary Cross-Servicing Agreement (TCSA) using the ACSA framework with Canada.

- **Other Bilateral Partners:**
  - *UK Royal Marines:* UK and US share NATO commonalities. The NATO SOFA (1951) and
    existing US-UK support agreements cover most needs. Marine units train alongside
    UK forces (e.g. Combined Amphibious Task Group exercises). Liaison via US Embassy
    DATT or Marine liaison sections.
  - *Australian Defence Force:* Governed by the 1951 ANZUS Treaty. ACSA agreements
    exist. Bilateral exercises (e.g. Talisman Sabre) use pre-negotiated logistics
    support through ACSA MOUs.
  - *Japanese Self-Defense Forces:* The 1960 US-Japan Security Treaty allows US
    forces to operate in Japan. Marines coordinate through the US Embassy's DAO and
    JSDF liaison channels.
  - *Philippine Marines:* The 1999 Philippines-US VFA and 2014 Enhanced Defense
    Cooperation Agreement (EDCA) govern US-Philippines training. EDCA specifies
    rotational bases. USMC coordinates with the Philippine Marine Corps and local
    navy.

- **Host-Nation Coordination:** Check customs/tax rules (often waived by SOFA),
  coordinate immigration/visas via the Embassy, and arrange base access passes. The
  local US Embassy (DAO, RSO, ALO) is the interface for diplomatic clearance. Marine
  planners must inform the DAO of major movements or civil projects. Engage local
  leadership early via civil affairs or defense attaches.

- **Practical Example:** A Reserve CA team attached to a MEU operates alongside
  Canadian forces and a host nation's military. Pre-deployment, they'd coordinate
  through the US Embassy: the Defense Attache negotiates a SOFA or visiting forces
  protocol. The CA team joins or establishes a CMOC to interface with NGOs and
  military. Communication nets include shared HF/VHF channels agreed with Canadians
  and a common data link. Fuel resupply flows under an ACSA. They use SOFA-derived
  privileges, liaise via the Embassy, and plug into multinational planning
  structures.

**Key References:** STANAG 2019/APP-6A (NATO symbology), NATO ATP/ADatP-3 series,
JP 3-16 (Multinational Operations), JP 6-0, CJCSI 6232.01F (Link-16), US-Philippines
VFA, US-Japan SOFA, Canada-US Bilateral Army Training Strategy.

## 3. Interagency (Other US Government Agencies)

- **Department of State:** All overseas USMC activities go through the State
  Department's Country Team. This includes the Ambassador, the Regional Security
  Officer (RSO), and the Defense Attache Office (DAO). The DAO (often a Marine O-6)
  is the primary military liaison, coordinating with local armed forces and Embassy
  security. A Marine exercise in-country requires Embassy approval via the DAO;
  planners brief the Country Team on their mission and request any State support.
  The RSO (Diplomatic Security) handles general security support. Requests for host
  nation support, legal queries, or NGO engagement are channeled through the
  Ambassador's staff and the DAO.

- **State Dept Humanitarian/Aid Structure (post-2025 USAID dissolution):**
  USAID is operationally defunct (dissolved 2025 via EO 14150/14169; functions
  absorbed into State by July 2025). Do not reference USAID as an active
  organization. Current structure:
  - **Under Secretary for Foreign Assistance & Humanitarian Affairs ("F")** —
    new position overseeing all aid bureaus
  - **Bureau for Humanitarian Assistance (BHA)** — from USAID/BHA; food, water,
    shelter, emergency medical, nutrition
  - **Bureau of Global Health Security & Diplomacy** — from USAID Global Health;
    HIV/AIDS, malaria, maternal/child health, PEPFAR
  - **Bureau of Disaster and Humanitarian Response (DHR)** — consolidates
    emergency relief; has rebuilt DART-equivalent capability
  - **PRM** (Population, Refugees, Migration) — unchanged, reports to "F"
  - **DRL** (Democracy, Human Rights & Religious Freedom) — absorbed USAID/DRG
  - **Food for Peace (Title II)** — transferred to **USDA**, not State
  - **Regional development** — State's geographic bureaus (AFR, EAP, EUR, NEA, WHA)

  DART status: All four active DARTs ceased operations Feb 2025. BHA staff cut from
  ~1,300 to ~50 embedded in PRM. DoD Civil Affairs units and military transport fill
  the interim disaster response gap. FY2026 humanitarian budget cut ~two-thirds
  (~$9.9B to ~$2.5B) — factor degraded civilian capacity into planning.

  JP 3-29 and JP 3-57 still reference USAID/OFDA as the US lead for foreign
  disaster relief — pending doctrinal update. Substitute State's humanitarian bureau
  wherever USAID appears. Confirm current status of any specific bureau before
  planning.

- **DHS/FEMA (Domestic Support):** DHS and FEMA lead domestic disaster response
  under the National Response Framework. Marine Reservists providing Defense Support
  to Civil Authorities (DSCA) typically operate under a *Dual-Status Command* during
  major incidents. By law (10 USC 331/32 USC 317), Governors activate National Guard
  (Title 32) while federal forces (Title 10) are sent in; a dual-status commander
  (DSC) commands both state and federal troops. JP 3-28 (DSCA) details how a DSC
  coordinates with FEMA and state authorities. Marines called to a hurricane or
  wildfire would coordinate through FEMA's Incident Command System as part of a joint
  military/civil task force. Normal law enforcement (e.g. riot control) is restricted
  by the Posse Comitatus Act; DoD support to civilian law enforcement requires
  specific authorizations (e.g. 10 USC 371, approved by SecDef).

- **Intelligence Community:** Marine units receive theater intelligence through the
  Combatant Command's J2 and service collection assets. The DNI oversees all agencies
  (CIA, DIA, NSA, etc.), but Marines request intel support via the MAGTF Intelligence
  Cell or joint intel centers (JICs). Classified intelligence sharing follows DNI
  guidelines; units must ensure releasability (NOFORN caveats, REL TO codes).

- **Law Enforcement Agencies:** Marines have very limited domestic law enforcement
  role under Posse Comitatus. Federal counter-narcotics support is authorized under
  10 USC 371 (requires SecDef approval), and military can train or advise DEA or ICE.
  Overseas, Marines may support host-nation forces fighting trafficking but must not
  be directly commanded by civilian law enforcement.

- **Other Agencies:**
  - **DOE** — leads tech response for nuclear/radiological incidents (Nuclear
    Incident Response Teams, NEST); military coordinates via FEMA (ESF #12)
  - **EPA** — leads environmental cleanup (ESF #10); Marine planners ensure hazmat
    handling per EPA guidelines
  - **HHS** — manages pandemic health response via National Disaster Medical System;
    Marines may provide logistics or security in support
  - **USDA** — now manages Food for Peace (Title II, ESF #11); food aid and nutrition
  In all cases, the lead federal agency (LFA) concept applies: Marines fall under
  the LFA's request and FEMA's coordinating framework for domestic response, or
  State Department channels for foreign relief.

- **Practical Example:** A USMC Reserve battalion and an Army brigade operate in a
  foreign port city during a combined exercise or humanitarian mission. Activities
  clear through the **US Embassy Country Team**: the DAO coordinates
  military-to-military contacts with the host, the DCM oversees interagency
  deconfliction. If State/DHR is also operating in-country, Marines embed a liaison
  with the State field office to sync logistics. Coordination with local NGOs goes
  through OCHA or the Embassy's Humanitarian Advisor. Marines coordinate *up*
  through DAO/ambassador and *across* via federal agency liaisons.

**Key References:** JP 3-08 (Interorganizational Cooperation), JP 3-57
(Civil-Military Operations), JP 3-28 (Defense Support to Civil Authorities),
32 USC 317 (Dual-Status Command), JP 3-29 (Foreign Humanitarian Assistance),
JP 2-0 (Joint Intelligence), DoD Instruction 3025.21 (DSCA).

## 4. NGO and International Organization Coordination

- **Key Humanitarian Organizations:** The UN's Office for the Coordination of
  Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) leads emergency response coordination. Relevant UN
  agencies: UNHCR (refugees), UNICEF (child welfare), WHO (health emergencies), WFP
  (food aid), OCHA/UNDAC (overall relief coordination). The ICRC operates in conflict
  zones under neutrality. The IFRC leads in natural disasters. NGOs vary widely
  (e.g. CARE, Mercy Corps, MSF), often organized under OCHA-led clusters. Each has
  its own mandate and principles; Marines must know who's doing what to avoid
  overlap.

- **Coordination Mechanisms:** In a disaster or stability operation, the military
  sets up a **Civil-Military Operations Center (CMOC)** or Humanitarian Assistance
  Coordination Cell (HACC) to interface with relief agencies. Per JP 3-57, a CMOC
  "may facilitate tactical-level CMO among the military, the local populace, NGOs,
  and IGOs." This means dedicating civil affairs Marines or a liaison officer to
  attend OCHA cluster meetings (logistics, health, shelter) and UN coordination
  groups. The UN **cluster system** divides efforts into sectors (e.g. Logistics
  Cluster led by WFP, Health by WHO). Marines should feed information (distribution
  schedules, force protection needs) into these clusters. The **Humanitarian Country
  Team (HCT)**, chaired by the UN Humanitarian Coordinator, provides high-level
  oversight. An **On-Site Operations Coordination Centre (OSOCC)** is established
  early for search-and-rescue where military and civilian responders register and
  share data.

- **NGO Perspectives:** Many NGOs emphasize **humanitarian principles**: humanity,
  neutrality, impartiality, and independence. They avoid appearing as military assets
  to maintain local trust and security. An NGO might refuse to ride in a Marine
  helicopter or use US markings at a relief camp to preserve neutral status. Military
  planners should coordinate through civilian leadership (e.g. OCHA), avoid
  co-branding relief projects, and limit armed presence around aid distributions.
  Trust is built by offering support (security or transport) without dominating the
  humanitarian agenda.

- **Common Structures:**
  - *Cluster System:* Sector working groups (e.g. WASH, shelter) chaired by UN
    agencies meet daily/weekly. Marines in logistics support liaise with the
    WFP-led Logistics Cluster to assign warehouses or transportation.
  - *Humanitarian Country Team (HCT):* Meeting of agency heads (UN, NGOs) that
    endorses strategic plans. A Marine task force leader may attend as an observer.
  - *On-Site Ops Coordination Center (OSOCC):* Example: after the 2010 Haiti
    earthquake, JTF-Haiti stood up a 30-person HACC integrated with the UN cluster
    system, with a Canadian general in charge. The HACC coordinated US military
    support to USAID, the UN, and the Haitian government.

- **Practical Example:** In a major disaster (typhoon zone with 15 NGOs), a Marine
  logistics unit reports to the CMOC where a civil affairs officer is the military
  representative. They attend the Logistics Cluster (usually led by WFP) and inform
  it of port or runway capabilities. Marines offer engineer or transport support
  (via MOA with State/DHR) and update NGO colleagues in daily SITREPs. Marines
  integrate into coordination centers — providing liaison officers and data rather
  than direct command.

**Key References:** JP 3-57 (Civil-Military Operations, especially CMOCs),
JP 3-08 (Interorganizational Coordination), IASC Guidelines on Cluster
Coordination, MCWP 3-33 series (Marine Corps Civil Affairs).

## 5. Communications & Liaison Quick Reference

- **Frequency Management:** Coordinate all nets through the joint spectrum
  management process. Obtain frequency authorizations via the J-6 or allied spectrum
  manager. In coalition ops, deconflict with host/partner nations. Common waveforms:
  **SINCGARS** (30-88 MHz VHF-FM), **HPW** (4-8 MHz HF with ALE), **SATCOM
  UHF/SHF**. File a Joint Restricted Frequency List (JRFL) for critical tactical
  nets. If allies don't share US keys, plan unencrypted backup nets (e.g. civilian
  SATphones).

- **PACE Plan Template:** Always construct a PACE communications plan:
  - **Primary:** Unit radios (e.g. PRC-117, PRC-152)
  - **Alternate:** Wideband SATCOM (AN/PRC-117F) or HF sets
  - **Contingency:** Satellite terminals (e.g. Iridium) or civilian channel
  - **Emergency:** Optical, UHF cross band, messenger/runner

  For a multinational exercise: Primary = joint HF network, Alternate = SATphone,
  Contingency = friendly civilian radio network (e.g. NATO HF net), Emergency =
  voice via runner or flag signals. Test each layer in advance.

- **Liaison Officer (LNO) Checklist:**
  - **Bring:** Authorization orders, working papers (order templates, terrain maps),
    radio/crypto gear, rank insignia, local language card, blank LOA/MOU forms,
    notebooks, SITREP formats
  - **Establish on arrival:** Billet accommodations, reporting chain, communications
    (exchange frequencies, call signs, codes), supported commander's SOPs (reporting
    times, frequency checks)
  - **Regularly report:** Higher commander's intent, plan changes, logistical
    status, issues for higher (casualties, supply shortfalls)

- **Call Signs & Reporting:** USMC and other services use different tactical call
  sign conventions. Marines use SALUTE/SITREP formats for enemy intel; Army uses
  specific OPREP formats. In coalitions, clarify call sign conventions early and
  agree on a common SITREP format for joint updates.

- **Classification Sharing:** Apply releasability markings: **NOFORN** (No Foreign
  Nationals) for US-only, **REL TO** (followed by country codes or alliance) to
  allow sharing. Common tags: REL NATO, FVEY (AUS/CAN/NZ/UK/US). Example: US
  SECRET releasable to NATO allies = **SECRET//REL TO NATO**. Five Eyes =
  **SECRET//FVEY**. Some coalition partners (non-NATO) may only receive UNCLASS if
  not covered by an agreement.

## 6. Agreements and Authorities Quick Reference

| Agreement Type | What It Covers | Who Signs | Lead Time | Template Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **SOFA** | Legal status of US forces in host country: jurisdiction, taxes, customs, base rights | Host government + US Ambassador; DoD/CJCS endorses | Months-years (treaty-level) | Dept of State treaty library; US Embassy archives |
| **VFA** | Like SOFA but for temporary deployments (training/exercises) | Host nation + SecDef (or Ambassador) | Weeks-months | State Dept templates; defense security cooperation manuals |
| **Technical Agreement (TA)** | Technical coordination: frequency deconfliction, fuel/ammo support, C2 interfaces | Component commanders or JTF/J-Staff, often with host nation rep | Weeks | Component SOPs; doctrinal annex examples |
| **MOU/MOA** | Mutual responsibilities for joint activities (training objectives, logistics sharing) | Mid-level commanders or agency heads; no treaty status | Weeks | Joint Publications (JP 3-57 annex); Service OJAs |
| **CSSA** | Domestic inter-service logistics support (e.g. using Army fuel for Marines) | Subordinate commanders or logistics chiefs (G-4/N4 level) | Days-weeks | Service logistics manuals; DLA guides |
| **ACSA** | International exchange of logistic support/supplies/services with allies | US SecDef + foreign defense minister (executive agreement) | Months-years | DoD ACSA Regulation (DODD 2010.9); DSCA website |

**Templates and precedents:**
- **SOFA/VFA:** Handled by State (e.g. NATO SOFA, US-PH VFA)
- **ACSAs:** Standardized DoD order forms (DD Form 448) and DoDACATS system
- **TAs/MOUs:** Often adapt Joint Pub annexes or STANAG procedures
- **CSSAs:** Army-Marine bilateral annexes; some forms under DLA guidance
