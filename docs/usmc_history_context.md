## Core Identity

The Marine Corps is a naval expeditionary force organized, trained, and equipped to operate from the sea, support naval campaigns, seize and defend advanced naval bases, conduct expeditionary operations, and respond rapidly to crises. A recurring historical theme is adaptation: Marines have served as shipboard detachments, landing forces, small-war constabularies, amphibious assault forces, expeditionary crisis responders, counterinsurgency forces, and stand-in forces.

Key identity phrases and concepts:

- Semper Fidelis: Always Faithful.
- Every Marine a rifleman: all Marines share basic warfighting identity and responsibility.
- First to fight: expeditionary readiness and crisis response.
- Once a Marine, always a Marine: enduring identity and ethos.
- Honor, Courage, Commitment: core values.
- Esprit de corps: shared pride, discipline, and belonging.

## Founding And Early Corps

### 1775: Birth Of The Marine Corps

The Continental Marines were established by the Continental Congress on November 10, 1775. Marine Corps tradition identifies Tun Tavern in Philadelphia as the birthplace of the Corps, associated with early recruiting by Captain Samuel Nicholas and Robert Mullan.

Why it matters:

- November 10 is celebrated as the Marine Corps Birthday.
- The founding links Marines to naval service, shipboard security, amphibious operations, and expeditionary tasks.
- Tun Tavern is part history, part institutional mythos: important as heritage, but worth understanding critically.

### Captain Samuel Nicholas

Samuel Nicholas is traditionally recognized as the first officer commissioned in the Continental Marines and the first Commandant by tradition.

### Early Naval And Expeditionary Role

Early Marines served aboard ships as guards, sharpshooters, landing parties, and disciplined naval infantry. This naval-expeditionary origin matters because modern Marine identity is still tied to operating with and from the Navy.

## Early Battles And Symbols

### 1805: Derna And The Barbary Wars

The Battle of Derna during the First Barbary War is remembered in the Marines' Hymn line "to the shores of Tripoli." Lieutenant Presley O'Bannon and Marines participated in an overland expedition that captured Derna.

Why it matters:

- Connects Marine history to expeditionary operations far from home.
- Often used as an early example of Marines supporting national interests abroad.
- Associated with the Mameluke sword tradition.

### 1847: Chapultepec

During the Mexican-American War, Marines fought at Chapultepec. The Marines' Hymn line "From the Halls of Montezuma" refers to this campaign.

Why it matters:

- Helps explain the Marines' Hymn.
- Connects Marine heritage to assault, discipline, and sacrifice.

### The Eagle, Globe, And Anchor

The emblem represents national service, worldwide reach, and naval tradition:

- Eagle: United States.
- Globe: worldwide service.
- Anchor: naval character and amphibious roots.

## Traditions, Customs, And Knowledge

### Marine Corps Birthday

The Birthday is celebrated on November 10. Birthday ceremonies commonly include a message from the Commandant, cake cutting, recognition of oldest and youngest Marines present, and reflection on the Corps' history.

### Marines' Hymn

The Marines' Hymn is the oldest official song in the U.S. armed services tradition. Its references to Montezuma and Tripoli connect current Marines to early expeditionary history.

### Core Values

- Honor: integrity, accountability, and ethical conduct.
- Courage: physical, moral, and mental strength to act rightly under pressure.
- Commitment: discipline, devotion to mission, and fidelity to Corps and country.

### Leadership Traits

Commonly memorized as JJ DID TIE BUCKLE:

- Justice
- Judgment
- Dependability
- Initiative
- Decisiveness
- Tact
- Integrity
- Enthusiasm
- Bearing
- Unselfishness
- Courage
- Knowledge
- Loyalty
- Endurance

### Leadership Principles

Commonly taught principles include:

- Know yourself and seek self-improvement.
- Be technically and tactically proficient.
- Know your Marines and look out for their welfare.
- Keep your Marines informed.
- Set the example.
- Ensure the task is understood, supervised, and accomplished.
- Train your Marines as a team.
- Make sound and timely decisions.
- Develop a sense of responsibility among subordinates.
- Employ your command in accordance with its capabilities.
- Seek responsibility and take responsibility for your actions.

## World War I

### 1918: Belleau Wood

At Belleau Wood, Marines fought in brutal combat during the German spring offensive. The battle became central to Marine Corps mythology and identity.

Why it matters:

- Often associated with the "Devil Dogs" nickname.
- Reinforced the Corps' reputation for marksmanship, aggressiveness, and tenacity.
- Became a defining modern Marine battle narrative.

Context:

The nickname and some quotes should be handled carefully because institutional memory, reporting, and legend sometimes blur. The larger truth is clear: Belleau Wood was costly, important, and identity-shaping.

## Between The World Wars

### Small Wars And The Banana Wars

Marines served in Haiti, the Dominican Republic, Nicaragua, and elsewhere. These operations involved expeditionary security, constabulary duty, local-force training, governance problems, and counterinsurgency-like challenges.

Why it matters:

- Led to lessons captured in the Small Wars Manual tradition.
- Shows that Marine history is not only major battles; it includes messy political-military operations.
- Useful for Civil Affairs, interagency, and advisory thinking.

### Amphibious Doctrine Development

Between the wars, Marines helped develop amphibious warfare doctrine that became decisive in World War II. This is one of the Corps' most important institutional adaptation stories.

Why it matters:

- The Corps preserved and refined a mission area that many considered too difficult.
- Doctrine, experimentation, and education shaped later operational success.
- Good PME case for innovation under resource and skepticism constraints.

## World War II

### Wake Island

Marines and other defenders resisted Japanese assault early in the Pacific War.

Why it matters:

- Symbol of early-war determination and sacrifice.
- Often used as an example of courage under isolation.

### Guadalcanal

Guadalcanal was the first major Allied offensive in the Pacific. Marines fought for control of Henderson Field and endured a long, difficult campaign.

Why it matters:

- Demonstrated expeditionary endurance and joint/naval integration.
- Highlighted logistics, air-ground coordination, and morale under pressure.

### Tarawa

Tarawa was a costly amphibious assault that exposed hard lessons in intelligence, tides, landing craft, communications, naval gunfire, and casualty evacuation.

Why it matters:

- Tactical victory with severe cost.
- Lessons informed later amphibious operations.
- Important reminder that doctrine must be tested against real conditions.

### Iwo Jima

The Battle of Iwo Jima became one of the most iconic Marine Corps battles. The flag raising on Mount Suribachi became a national symbol.

Why it matters:

- Associated with Admiral Chester Nimitz's statement that uncommon valor was a common virtue.
- Demonstrates sacrifice, fortification reduction, combined arms, and morale.
- Symbolic power can overshadow tactical complexity; study both.

### Okinawa

Okinawa was one of the largest and bloodiest Pacific battles, involving prolonged ground combat, joint operations, and heavy casualties.

Why it matters:

- Shows the human and operational cost of late-war Pacific campaigns.
- Highlights logistics, endurance, and joint/naval support.

## Korean War

### 1950: Inchon

The landing at Inchon was a bold amphibious operation that helped reverse the strategic situation after the Pusan Perimeter crisis.

Why it matters:

- Amphibious surprise and operational audacity.
- Demonstrates naval integration and risk acceptance.

### 1950: Chosin Reservoir

Marines and other U.N. forces fought in extreme cold against Chinese forces around the Chosin Reservoir and conducted a fighting withdrawal.

Why it matters:

- Central Marine story of discipline, endurance, cohesion, and tactical skill under severe conditions.
- Often summarized as "attacking in another direction."
- Good leadership case for logistics, weather, morale, casualty care, and command under pressure.

## Vietnam War

### Khe Sanh

Khe Sanh involved a prolonged siege and major air/logistics support effort.

Why it matters:

- Shows fire support, air resupply, defensive operations, and strategic attention.
- Useful for discussing the gap between tactical action and strategic interpretation.

### 1968: Hue City

During the Tet Offensive, Marines fought house-to-house in Hue.

Why it matters:

- Major urban combat case.
- Highlights combined arms, small-unit leadership, civilian presence, intelligence gaps, and the complexity of fighting in cities.
- Important comparison point for later urban battles such as Fallujah.

## Beirut, Grenada, Panama, And The Late Cold War

### Beirut, 1983

The Beirut barracks bombing killed 241 U.S. service members, including 220 Marines.

Why it matters:

- Force protection, rules of engagement, mission clarity, and terrorism.
- Somber reminder of vulnerability in peacekeeping and security missions.

### Grenada And Panama

Marines participated in contingency operations during the 1980s.

Why it matters:

- Crisis response, joint operations, and expeditionary readiness.

## Gulf War And Post-Cold War Operations

### Desert Shield / Desert Storm

Marines played major roles in the liberation of Kuwait and operations against Iraqi forces.

Why it matters:

- Large-scale maneuver, combined arms, deception, and coalition operations.
- Shows Marine Corps relevance after Vietnam and during the post-Cold War transition.

### Humanitarian Assistance And Disaster Relief

Marines have repeatedly supported humanitarian and disaster response missions.

Why it matters:

- Expeditionary readiness is not limited to combat.
- Logistics, communications, civil-military coordination, and disciplined presence matter.

## Iraq And Afghanistan

### Afghanistan / Operation Enduring Freedom

Marines conducted combat, security, advising, and stabilization missions.

Why it matters:

- Counterinsurgency, distributed operations, partner-force advising, and endurance across long campaigns.

### Iraq / Operation Iraqi Freedom

Marines participated in the invasion of Iraq and subsequent security/counterinsurgency operations.

### 2004: Fallujah

The Second Battle of Fallujah / Operation Phantom Fury was one of the major urban battles of the Iraq War.

Why it matters:

- Modern urban combat, combined arms, small-unit leadership, information operations, civilian considerations, and tactical adaptation.
- Often compared with Hue City for urban warfare study.

## Modern Marine Corps Themes

### Force Design And Stand-In Forces

Recent Marine Corps thinking emphasizes naval integration, littoral operations, expeditionary advanced base operations, sensing, fires, mobility, and operating inside contested environments.

Why it matters:

- Shows continued adaptation to strategic conditions.
- Connects old expeditionary identity to modern naval campaigning.

### Reserves In Marine History

Reserve Marines have repeatedly augmented, reinforced, and sustained the Corps in major conflicts and contingency operations. For SMCR users, history is not just active-component heritage; reserve readiness, mobilization, training discipline, and continuity between drills are part of the Corps' ability to respond.

## Marines Commonly Studied As Examples

### Dan Daly

Two-time Medal of Honor recipient associated with Belleau Wood lore. Useful for discussions of courage, aggressiveness, and the line between legend and documented history.

### Smedley D. Butler

Two-time Medal of Honor recipient, later a public critic of war and militarism. Useful for ethics, civil-military discussion, and the complexity of Marine heritage.

### Chesty Puller

Lieutenant General Lewis "Chesty" Puller is one of the Corps' most famous Marines, associated with aggressive leadership, combat experience, and Marine identity.

### John Basilone

Medal of Honor recipient for Guadalcanal and Navy Cross recipient for Iwo Jima. Often studied for courage, sacrifice, and leadership.

### Ira Hayes, Rene Gagnon, Harlon Block, Franklin Sousley, Michael Strank, Harold Schultz

Associated with the Iwo Jima flag raising. Their stories are useful for discussing memory, symbolism, individual sacrifice, and the difference between iconic imagery and lived experience.

### Jason Dunham, Dakota Meyer, Kyle Carpenter, John Canley

Modern examples of conspicuous gallantry and leadership under fire, commonly used in leadership and values discussions.
