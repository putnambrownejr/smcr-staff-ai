# Reserve Life: What They Don't Teach You at TBS (UNCLASSIFIED)

## The Reserve Mindset Shift

If you're coming from active duty, the biggest adjustment isn't the workload — it's the rhythm. You go from 100% Marine to a Marine who happens to have a civilian life, a civilian boss, and 28 days between drills where your Marines are scattered across the state.

If you're commissioning directly into the reserves, you're stepping into a world where "good initiative, bad judgment" has a different cost — your Marines used PTO and drove 3 hours to be here. Don't waste their time.

## Key Differences from Active Duty

| Active Duty | Reserves |
|-------------|----------|
| See Marines daily | See Marines 2 days/month + AT |
| Admin handled by full-time staff | Admin shared between you, I&I, and the Marine |
| Training schedule = your life | Training competes with civilian career |
| CO can task you anytime | CO has 48 drill days to accomplish the mission |
| Promotion = performance + timing | Promotion = performance + PME + board scores + timing |
| Housing/TRICARE automatic | TRICARE Reserve Select requires enrollment + premiums |

## Benefits You Should Know About

### Pay
- **IDT pay**: 1/30th of monthly base pay per drill period (4 periods per weekend = 2 days' pay)
- **AT pay**: Full base pay for the duration of AT orders
- **Travel reimbursement**: Up to $750/month for IDT travel (verify current MARADMIN)
- **Check your LES**: Log into MOL monthly and verify your pay is correct

### Healthcare
- **TRICARE Reserve Select (TRS)**: Available to all drilling reservists; requires enrollment and premiums
- **TRICARE Dental**: Separate enrollment through FEDVIP
- **Line of Duty injuries**: If you're injured during drill or AT, report it immediately — this is a line-of-duty determination

### Education
- **Montgomery GI Bill - Selected Reserve (MGIB-SR)**: Monthly stipend while enrolled in school
- **Tuition Assistance**: Available for drilling reservists (check current policy)
- **Federal and state tuition benefits**: Vary by state — some states offer significant tuition waivers

### Retirement
- **Reserve retirement**: Eligible at age 60 with 20+ qualifying years of service
- **Good year**: Must have 50+ retirement points in a fiscal year to count as a qualifying year
- **Points breakdown**: 15 membership points + 1 per IDT + 1 per AT day + correspondence/PME points

## USERRA: Your Employment Protection

The Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act (USERRA) protects your civilian job:

- Your employer cannot fire you, deny promotion, or discriminate because of military service
- You have reemployment rights after military duty
- You must give advance notice to your employer (written preferred, verbal acceptable)
- You should return to work promptly after drill/AT (next scheduled work day is typical)

**Best practice**: Give your employer your drill schedule for the year up front. Communicate early about AT dates. Most employer friction comes from surprise, not the absence itself.

## Practical Tips from Experience

1. **Keep a go-bag ready.** Uniform, boots, cover, notebook, pen, CAC, extra socks. You will forget something eventually — minimize what it could be.

2. **Set calendar reminders.** Drill weekends, DTS voucher deadlines, PME enrollment windows, PFT/CFT dates. If it's not on your calendar, it won't happen.

3. **Build a drill binder.** Physical or digital — contains your training schedule, recall roster, SOP, and any products you're working on. Bring it every drill.

4. **Know your I&I by name.** The Inspector-Instructor staff is your lifeline between drills. A good relationship with your I&I counterpart makes everything easier.

5. **File your DTS voucher on Sunday.** Before you drive home. Once you leave the drill site, the odds of filing drop to about 50%.

6. **Track your own records.** Don't assume the system is right. Check your MOL, verify your training record, review your OMPF annually. If something's wrong, you'll catch it faster than anyone else.

7. **Stay connected between drills.** Check email weekly. Respond to taskers. Show up to virtual meetings. The Marines who go dark between drills are the ones who fall behind — and their Marines notice.

8. **Plan AT early.** Your Marines have civilian jobs too. The more lead time you give them, the fewer "I can't get off work" problems you'll have.
