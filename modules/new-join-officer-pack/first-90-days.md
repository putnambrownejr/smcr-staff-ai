# New Reserve Officer: First 90 Days Checklist (UNCLASSIFIED)

## Before Your First Drill (Week 1-2)

### Administrative
- [ ] Report to I&I (Inspector-Instructor staff) — they're your day-to-day admin point of contact
- [ ] Verify your orders are correct (unit, billet, reporting date)
- [ ] Get added to unit distribution list (email, Teams/GroupMe, whatever the unit uses)
- [ ] Obtain unit recall roster and key leader contact info
- [ ] Get a copy of the unit training schedule (at least next 3 months)
- [ ] Confirm your CAC is current and PKI certificates are loaded

### System Access
- [ ] MOL (Marine Online) — verify you can log in and your data is correct
- [ ] DTS (Defense Travel System) — set up your profile and routing list
- [ ] MarineNet — verify access, check for overdue annual training
- [ ] Unit SharePoint/shared drive — get access from S-6 or I&I
- [ ] Government email — check if you have a .mil account or need one

### Intelligence / Security
- [ ] Verify your clearance status with the S-2 / security manager
- [ ] If clearance is expired or in adjudication, start the process immediately
- [ ] Complete any required indoctrination briefings

## First Drill Weekend (Month 1)

### People to Meet
- [ ] Commanding Officer — introduce yourself, understand their priorities
- [ ] Executive Officer — your primary staff mentor; ask about due-outs and battle rhythm
- [ ] First Sergeant / Sergeant Major — they know the unit's real state
- [ ] Your predecessor (if available) — get the turnover binder
- [ ] I&I counterpart in your section — they keep things moving between drills
- [ ] Adjacent staff officers — understand how your section connects to theirs

### Things to Learn
- [ ] Unit mission statement and METL (Mission Essential Task List)
- [ ] Current training plan and where the unit is in its training cycle
- [ ] Readiness status — what's green, what's red, why?
- [ ] Standing operating procedures — ask for the SOP binder
- [ ] Upcoming events — next AT, exercises, inspections, ceremonies

### Things to Do
- [ ] Attend all formations — be early
- [ ] Take notes on everything — names, processes, jargon you don't know
- [ ] Don't try to change anything yet — observe and learn
- [ ] Volunteer for a task that lets you learn the staff process

## First 30 Days

- [ ] FitRep setup: identify your Reporting Officer (RO) and Reviewing Officer
- [ ] Get counseled on performance expectations by your RO
- [ ] Review your OMPF (Official Military Personnel File) on MOL for accuracy
- [ ] Verify PFT/CFT scores are current; schedule if not
- [ ] Verify rifle qualification is current; schedule if not
- [ ] Complete all overdue annual training on MarineNet
- [ ] Set up DTS for any upcoming travel (next drill if you travel > 50 miles)
- [ ] Read the unit's last 2-3 AARs to understand what went well/poorly
- [ ] Identify your PME requirements for next promotion (see PME planning doc)

## 30-60 Days

- [ ] Start producing in your billet — own a deliverable for next drill
- [ ] Build relationships with your NCOs — they know how things actually work
- [ ] Attend a staff meeting and understand the staff estimate process
- [ ] Draft your section's contribution to the unit training schedule
- [ ] Start your due-out tracker — things you owe and things owed to you
- [ ] If on a staff: learn to brief the commander (concise, recommendation up front)

## 60-90 Days

- [ ] You should be running your section's drill weekend activities independently
- [ ] Present at a staff meeting or training brief
- [ ] Complete your initial counseling and have a development plan
- [ ] Know your Marines by name and know their readiness status
- [ ] Have a handle on the admin systems you use (Drill Mgr, DTS, MROWS, MOL)
- [ ] Start thinking about AT planning for the next cycle

## Common New-Join Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Trying to fix everything at once.** Observe for 2-3 drills before proposing changes. The unit's way of doing things usually has a reason — ask why before suggesting different.

2. **Ignoring the I&I staff.** They're the continuity. They know what works and what's been tried. Treat them as partners, not subordinates.

3. **Assuming active-duty timelines.** In the reserves, you have 48 days to do what active duty does in 365. Everything takes longer because it crosses drill-to-drill gaps.

4. **Not filing DTS vouchers.** If you travel to drill and don't file a voucher, you're leaving money on the table AND creating an admin headache later.

5. **Forgetting about PME.** Promotion board precepts care about PME completion. Start early — courses fill up and scheduling around civilian jobs is hard.

6. **Going dark between drills.** Check email weekly at minimum. Respond to taskers. The Marines who disappear between drills are the ones who fall behind.

*UNCLASSIFIED — advisory checklist. Adapt to your specific unit, billet, and situation.*
